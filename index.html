WIP CONCEPT
01 The Soul
Offering more hands for tangible human progress.
In the last few years, AI has already made tremendous progress across language, vision and other domains with structure. But the physical world is different. No two moments are the same. No two surfaces, objects, or situations combine in quite the same way. The variables are effectively infinite. You need intelligence that can act in situations it has never encountered before. So it can handle the world with care.
02 The Hard Problem
Why it's hard
Folding laundry, packing boxes. Tasks that look routine to humans are genuinely novel physical problems solved hundreds of times an hour. Millions of years of evolution made humans extraordinary physical reasoners. For machines, every instance is a first.
Why generalisation is everything
Real-world value requires intelligence that meets the world as it actually is, not constrained to certain forms or tasks. That's what π is building. Not narrow capability for known situations, but physical reasoning broad enough to be trusted anywhere.
03 Civilisational Frame
The problems that matter most in the coming century aren't problems of information. They're problems of presence. Shrinking populations, growing physical demands, and a hard demographic ceiling on how many human hands exist where they're needed.
Elderly care
Healthcare delivery
Disaster relief
Agriculture
Climate infrastructure
Underserved communities
Early childhood
What these share: unambiguous need, structural labor shortage that gets worse not better, and recipients who deserve more than they're getting. There is more work that needs doing than hands to do it.

05 Content Strategy — Four Layers
Research voice
Stays exactly as it is. Precise, evidence-forward, peer-facing. Complemented by video content that surfaces the details — making the performance and significance legible to even non-roboticist audience.
Company voice
Start building a coherent company perspective on a civilisation coexisting with physical intelligence, through about pages, founder interviews, podcasts, long-form pieces and more.
Cultural voice
A curious exploration of the history of physical labour, how humans have imagined machines across culture and time, and what the world's unmet physical needs actually look like today.
Product voice
As developer platform product, π presents robustness at the face of different situations. Consider the opportunities to shape the motion language of machines powered by π. That could be a stepping stone for future consumer product.
06 Tangible Warmth
Building a company that makes a lasting impression, across candidates and the general public, requires having a point of view. Not the cold minimalism of enterprise software. Not the glossy optimism of consumer tech. In a world of transition, Physical Intelligence embodies a more grounded perspective. As if the brand itself has weight, made by people who care deeply about the physical world. The visual language should carry that quality: tangible, deliberate, unhurried. A company that understands presence.
Every enduring brand has a colour it owns. Physical Intelligence needs that signal, a single ownable hue that anchors the palette and becomes the shorthand for the brand at a glance. Sage carries the right associations: intelligence, yet real. It isn't a tech colour or a neutral.
The palette manifests across physical merch to digital content. It reads as considered, ages well, and holds its character from a video backdrop to an embroidered chest logo.
07 Scenes of Wonder
For the visual storytelling direction, there is an opportunity to share a whimsical glimpse of a world where robots are slotted into the odd and ordinary moments of everyday life. Unlike most AI products, robots are already so deeply ingrained into our cultural awareness. We can lean hard into our core research premise of being able to act in the face of novel situations and tease out the odd and magical moments of everyday life.
08 Clarity as Beauty
Robotics is something very visceral, but coherent motion and actions are often taken for granted. Our research video content strategy can mirror a director's commentary, bringing in researchers and partners to annotate details that may be missed by ordinary eyes. This gives a very digestible unit for people, even those not deep in robotics, to consume.
When it comes to presenting statistics, data visualisation should be clear with minimal visual distractions. Strip the hard angular shadows and always annotate the implications of different data on developers or users.
